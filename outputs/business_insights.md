# Business Insights

## Overview

The Tableau executive dashboard analyzes retail sales performance across sales, profit, profit margin, customer segment behavior, category performance, discount impact, shipping performance, and return patterns. The dashboard is designed for a retail leadership team that needs to understand not only where the business is performing well, but also where risks are affecting profitability and customer experience.

The overall dashboard KPIs show that the business has a strong sales base and is profitable overall. However, the detailed charts show that performance is not equal across regions, categories, customer segments, discount levels, and shipping modes.

| Metric | Value |
|---|---:|
| Total Sales | `$217.02M` |
| Total Profit | `$33.31M` |
| Profit Margin | `15.35%` |
| Total Orders | `4,200` |
| Return Rate | `4.55%` |

## Insight 1: Sales Performance Is Strong Overall, But Monthly Sales Fluctuate

Observation:

The sales trend view shows that the business generates consistent sales across the two-year period, but monthly sales are not completely stable. Some months perform noticeably better than others, which suggests that the business may experience seasonal demand, campaign-driven changes, or category-specific sales cycles.

Data evidence:

The business generated approximately `$217.02M` in total sales across 4,200 orders. Monthly sales range from approximately `$6.30M` in August 2024 to approximately `$10.86M` in August 2025. This difference shows that sales volume changes meaningfully across months rather than staying flat.

Business interpretation:

The overall sales number is strong, but leadership should not rely only on the total sales KPI. Month-by-month movement matters because it can reveal demand cycles, campaign effectiveness, and operational pressure points. If high-sales months are linked to specific campaigns or categories, those activities can be repeated. If low-sales months are recurring, they may need targeted promotions or inventory planning.

Recommended action or follow-up question:

Analyze monthly sales by category, region, and campaign channel to identify what drives sales peaks and sales drops. A useful follow-up question is: Are high-sales months caused by stronger demand, higher discounts, seasonal campaigns, or specific product categories?

## Insight 2: South Is The Strongest Region By Sales

Observation:

The Regional Performance chart shows that South is the highest-sales region. This means South contributes the largest share of business revenue compared with the other regions.

Data evidence:

South generated approximately `$64.69M` in sales and `$9.99M` in profit. This is higher than North, which generated approximately `$54.56M` in sales, West at `$48.91M`, and East at `$48.86M`.

Business interpretation:

South is a key revenue market for the business. Its higher sales may be due to stronger customer demand, better product availability, stronger regional campaigns, or a more favorable category mix. Because South contributes the highest sales, changes in South’s performance can significantly affect overall company performance.

Recommended action or follow-up question:

Leadership should study South in more detail to understand what is driving its strong sales. The next step should be to compare South against other regions by category mix, customer segment, discount level, and campaign channel. If South has successful practices, those can be adapted for lower-sales regions.

## Insight 3: East Has The Best Profit Margin Among Regions

Observation:

Although South is the strongest region by sales, East has the highest profit margin. This means East is slightly better at converting sales into profit.

Data evidence:

East has a profit margin of approximately `15.55%`, while South has `15.44%`, North has `15.24%`, and West has `15.14%`. The difference is not huge, but East is still the best-performing region from a margin perspective.

Business interpretation:

This shows that the highest-sales region is not automatically the most profitable region by margin. East may have a better product mix, lower discounts, lower costs, or stronger sales of high-margin categories. This is useful for leadership because it separates revenue performance from profit efficiency.

Recommended action or follow-up question:

Compare East and South to understand why East has better margin while South has higher sales. Leadership should investigate whether East sells more Technology products, uses fewer high discounts, or has a different customer segment mix.

## Insight 4: Technology Is The Main Profit Driver

Observation:

Technology is the strongest product category in the dashboard. It leads in both sales and profit, making it the most important category for business performance.

Data evidence:

Technology generated approximately `$153.90M` in sales and `$28.04M` in profit. Its profit margin is approximately `18.22%`. This is much stronger than Furniture, which generated `$51.64M` in sales and `$3.56M` in profit, and Office Supplies, which generated `$11.48M` in sales and `$1.71M` in profit.

Business interpretation:

Technology is the main engine of profitability. It is not only generating the highest sales, but also converting those sales into profit at a strong margin. This makes Technology a category that leadership should protect and grow. Any supply issue, pricing mistake, or inventory shortage in Technology could have a large impact on total profit.

Recommended action or follow-up question:

Prioritize Technology in inventory planning, supplier negotiations, and marketing campaigns. A useful follow-up is to check whether Technology sales are driven by a few products or spread across multiple sub-categories.

## Insight 5: Technology Sub-Categories Dominate Profitability

Observation:

The Category Profitability view shows that the top profit-generating sub-categories are all from Technology. This means Technology’s strength is broad across several sub-categories, not limited to only one product group.

Data evidence:

The top Technology sub-categories by profit are Copiers at approximately `$7.31M`, Accessories at `$7.19M`, Phones at `$7.10M`, and Machines at `$6.45M`.

Business interpretation:

This is a positive sign because the business is not dependent on only one Technology sub-category. Multiple Technology sub-categories are contributing strong profit, which reduces concentration risk within the category. It also suggests that Technology customers may have strong demand across different product needs.

Recommended action or follow-up question:

Leadership should review product availability, pricing, and campaign performance for Copiers, Accessories, Phones, and Machines. The next analysis should identify whether these sub-categories also have lower return rates and lower discount dependency.

## Insight 6: Furniture Has Lower Profitability And Higher Return Risk

Observation:

Furniture is a risk area because it has a much lower profit margin and a higher return rate than the other categories. Even though Furniture contributes a meaningful amount of sales, it does not generate profit as efficiently.

Data evidence:

Furniture generated approximately `$51.64M` in sales but only `$3.56M` in profit. Its profit margin is approximately `6.89%`, compared with `18.22%` for Technology and `14.85%` for Office Supplies. Furniture also has the highest category return rate at approximately `7.67%`.

Business interpretation:

Furniture may be creating hidden costs for the business. A high return rate can lead to reverse logistics costs, product damage, refunds, replacement costs, customer service workload, and lower customer satisfaction. The low margin means the business has less room to absorb these costs.

Recommended action or follow-up question:

Investigate Furniture returns by sub-category, customer segment, region, and shipping mode. Leadership should check whether the return problem is linked to product quality, delivery damage, incorrect product expectations, or heavy discounting.

## Insight 7: Home Office Is Valuable But Has The Highest Return Rate

Observation:

Home Office is the highest-sales customer segment, but it also has the highest return rate. This makes it both a valuable and risky segment.

Data evidence:

Home Office generated approximately `$74.50M` in sales and `$11.56M` in profit. Its return rate is approximately `5.67%`, which is higher than Consumer at `3.91%` and Corporate at `4.00%`.

Business interpretation:

Home Office customers are important because they generate the most sales and profit among segments. However, the higher return rate suggests that this segment may have different buying behavior or expectations. It may also be purchasing more products from higher-return categories like Furniture.

Recommended action or follow-up question:

Analyze Home Office returns by category and sub-category. A useful follow-up question is: Are Home Office returns mainly driven by Furniture purchases, delivery delays, or specific regions?

---

## Insight 8: High Discounts Are Reducing Profitability

Observation:

The Discount vs Profit view shows that higher discount levels are linked with weaker profitability. High-discount sales may create revenue, but they do not produce strong margins.

Data evidence:

No-discount orders have a profit margin of approximately `20.56%`. Low-discount orders have a profit margin of `17.01%`. Medium-discount orders fall to `13.63%`, and high-discount orders fall further to only `7.45%`. High-discount orders also have a higher return rate of approximately `6.09%`.

Business interpretation:

Discounting appears to be a major profitability risk. If discounts are too aggressive, the business may be sacrificing margin without enough benefit. The higher return rate for high-discount orders also suggests that these sales may be more risky or less stable.

Recommended action or follow-up question:

Review discount strategy by category and campaign channel. Leadership should check whether high discounts are creating incremental sales or simply reducing profit on orders that may have happened without the discount.

## Insight 9: Return Pattern Is Highest In Furniture And Home Office

Observation:

Return risk is not evenly distributed across the business. The dashboard shows that return risk is more concentrated in Furniture and in the Home Office segment.

Data evidence:

Furniture has the highest category return rate at approximately `7.67%`. Home Office has the highest customer segment return rate at approximately `5.67%`. The overall return rate is `4.55%`, which means these areas are above the business average.

Business interpretation:

The business-wide return rate can hide important risk pockets. Furniture and Home Office should be reviewed together because their return rates are both high. If Home Office customers are returning Furniture products more often, this could point to expectation mismatch, product suitability issues, or delivery-related problems.

Recommended action or follow-up question:

Review Furniture returns among Home Office customers by sub-category, region, shipping mode, and delivery days. This will help identify whether the issue is product-related, customer-related, or fulfillment-related.

## Insight 10: Standard Class Has The Longest Delivery Time

Observation:

Standard Class has the longest average delivery time among shipping modes and also carries the largest sales volume.

Data evidence:

Standard Class has an average delivery time of approximately `4.71` days and sales of approximately `$122.83M`. Second Class averages `2.68` days, First Class averages `1.77` days, and Same Day averages `0.40` days.

Business interpretation:

Standard Class is operationally important because it handles the largest portion of sales. Even small improvements in Standard Class delivery performance could affect a large number of customers. Although Standard Class does not have the highest return rate, long delivery times can still influence customer satisfaction and repeat purchase behavior.

Recommended action or follow-up question:

Monitor Standard Class delivery performance by region, category, and customer segment. Leadership should check whether Standard Class delays are concentrated in specific regions or product categories.

---

## Insight 11: Delayed Orders Should Be Monitored Even If Severe Delays Are Low

Observation:

Most orders are not severely delayed, but delayed orders still represent an operational risk that should be monitored.

Data evidence:

The `Delayed: 6-8 days` bucket contains 660 orders and has a return rate of approximately `4.85%`. The `Severely Delayed: 9+ days` bucket contains only 6 orders, so that group should be interpreted carefully due to very low volume.

Business interpretation:

Severe delays are rare in the dataset, which is positive. However, the delayed bucket still includes a meaningful number of orders. Delivery delays may not always directly cause returns, but they can affect customer satisfaction, ratings, and future loyalty.

Recommended action or follow-up question:

Combine delivery delay analysis with customer rating and return data. A useful next step is to check whether delayed orders have lower customer ratings or higher return rates in specific categories.

---

## Insight 12: Best Business Opportunity Is Profitable Technology Growth With Controlled Discounting

Observation:

The strongest opportunity is to grow Technology sales while controlling discount leakage. Technology already performs well, while high-discount orders reduce profitability.

Data evidence:

Technology contributes approximately `$28.04M` in profit with a margin of `18.22%`. High-discount orders have a much lower profit margin of `7.45%`, compared with `20.56%` for no-discount orders.

Business interpretation:

The business can improve profit by focusing on high-margin categories and reducing unnecessary discounting. Technology appears to have strong demand and profitability, so broad discounting may not be needed for all Technology products.

Recommended action or follow-up question:

Create category-specific discount rules. Leadership should avoid using the same discount strategy across all categories and should test whether discounts are actually needed for high-performing Technology sub-categories.
